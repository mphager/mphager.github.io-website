

## Overview
In this project, I analyzed COMP110 survey data to explore whether quizzes and other practice-based learning tools are associated with stronger student understanding.

## Idea Analyzed
I focused on the idea that the course should incorporate more practice problems similar to quizzes and exams. The reasoning behind this is that repeated practice helps reinforce key concepts and may improve overall understanding.

---

## Visualization 1: Distribution of Quiz Effectiveness Ratings

This bar chart shows how students rated the effectiveness of quizzes on a scale from 1 to 7. Most responses fall between 5 and 7, which suggests that students generally view quizzes as helpful.

![Distribution of quiz ratings](graph1.png)

While this doesn’t directly prove quizzes improve understanding, it does show that students perceive them as valuable, which is important when thinking about course design.

---

## Visualization 2: High Quiz Effectiveness vs Understanding

This boxplot focuses only on students who rated quiz effectiveness highly (5 or above). It shows how their understanding scores are distributed.

![High quiz effectiveness filtered data](graph2.png)

Even among students who think quizzes are effective, understanding still varies quite a bit. This suggests that while quizzes likely help, they are not the only factor influencing how well students understand the material.

---

## Visualization 3: Quiz Effectiveness vs Programming Assignment Effectiveness

This scatterplot compares how students rated quizzes versus programming assignments.

![Quiz vs programming effectiveness](graph3.png)

There is a general trend where students who rate quizzes highly also tend to rate programming assignments highly. This suggests that students don’t see these tools as competing, but rather as complementary parts of learning.

---

## Conclusion

Overall, the data suggests that quizzes are a useful tool and are associated with higher perceived understanding. However, the relationship is not very strong, and there is still a lot of variation. This means quizzes alone are not enough to fully support student success.

Because of this, I would recommend adding more structured practice opportunities, but keeping them low-stakes so they don’t increase stress too much. The data also suggests that combining quizzes with other tools, like programming assignments, is likely the most effective approach rather than relying on just one method.
